Doctoral Research Student

Thesis Title: Population-scale plasma proteomics for preclinical detection, causal target nomination, and non-invasive precision endotyping of chronic kidney disease.

Chronic kidney disease (CKD) is a progressive loss of nephron mass through glomerular, tubular, and interstitial injury that affects over 750 million people globally. It is estimated that 90% of individuals with stage 3 CKD (kidney function ~30–59% capacity) are unaware of their condition because early-stage disease is often asymptomatic.

Using UK Biobank and Kidney Precision Medicine Program cohorts, I am developing and evaluating proteomic ML models for (I) subclinical CKD detection, (II) CKD proteomics causal inference (Mendelian randomization pQTL), and (III) tracing the cell-of-origin for causal biomarkers. Ultimately, this work aims to enable precision nephrology in CKD management by leveraging non-invasive plasma proteomic liquid biopsy.

ML Research Assistant

Goal: Develop a best-in-class AI-assisted clinical decision making tool for maternal preeclampsia.

Preeclampsia (PE) is a maternal hypertensive disorder that claims the lives of 50,000 mothers and 500,000 babies annually. Because its symptoms are very generic, early and accurate detection of PE is a difficult task.

I developed a novel AI diagnostic aid and maternal risk stratification tool for preterm PE diagnosis to assist clinicians tailor patient care strategies (i.e aspirin prophylaxis). I improved model sensitivity and recall (PPV) by >10% for PE status prediction and developed and evaluated a low-cost clinical severity score, based on polygenic risk scores, which demonstrated strong correlation with physician-recorded severity status. Feature analysis validated significant contributions of novel, high-specificity biomarkers (patent pending) for PE diagnosis.

Undergraduate Research Assistant

Goal: Determine the mechanism of action for the TGF-β/smad peroxide resistance response in C. elegans as a model for neuronal resilience in Alzheimer's Disease.

I performed lifespan and behavioral assays on type I and type II TGF-β ligand/receptor RNAi nematode knockouts during peroxide exposure, observing a signal integration phenotype that causes half of double ligand knockouts to live longer than wildtype.

I then analyzed publicly available bulk RNA-seq data from C. elegans smad knockouts to trace differential gene expression from TGF-β silencing. I discovered that both smad-2 and smad-4 knockouts overexpressed lon-1, which is found abundantly in peroxide-sensing neurons and implicated in negative regulation of the TGF-β pathway.
